On-Ground & Feeding

The heart of Pawsitive. Present every single day.

what we do

This team oversees year-round feeding operations for dogs within the club’s jurisdiction, coordinating student schedules during the semester and ensuring continuity of care during vacation periods. They respond to medical emergencies involving dogs and cats, maintain stocked medical kits, and ensure medications are administered on schedule. The team also keeps an updated census of animals under care and coordinates veterinary visits and follow-ups as needed.

responsibilities

  • Year-round feeding operations twice daily, coordinating student schedules including during vacation periods
  • Responding to medical emergencies — first assessment, contacting vets, transporting animals if needed
  • Maintaining stocked first-aid kits and medication schedules for all animals under care
  • Maintaining an updated census of all animals — names, health records, feeding notes, and status
  • Coordinating all veterinary visits, follow-up appointments, and post-treatment care
  • Monitoring behavioural and health changes in campus animals and flagging concerns early

past highlights

Blanket Drive

As winter temperatures in Sonipat, Haryana dropped to single digits, Pawsitive organized a Blanket Drive to help campus dogs withstand the cold. Through this initiative, warm blankets were distributed across the campus to provide the dogs with protection and comfort during the harsh winter nights.

Collar Drive

To improve the safety of the campus dogs, Pawsitive organized a Reflective Collar Drive, through which reflective collars were fitted on dogs across the campus. These collars increase the dogs' visibility to drivers at night and in low-light conditions, significantly reducing their susceptibility to vehicular accidents.

Daily Feeding Drives

Pawsitive conducts daily feeding drives to ensure that the campus dogs are consistently provided with nutritious meals. The dogs are fed twice a day, once in the afternoon and again at night, by trained members of the club. The food distributed during these drives primarily consists of leftover meals from Ashoka University's dining facilities, allowing the club to reduce food wastage while ensuring that the dogs do not go hungry. These meals typically include items such as rice, roti, dal, chicken, and eggs, providing the dogs with a balanced and reliable source of nourishment.

Medication Management and Treatment Monitoring

When a campus dog is prescribed medication by a veterinarian, Pawsitive ensures that the treatment is administered consistently and responsibly. Volunteers coordinate to provide the correct dosage at the appropriate intervals, closely following the prescribed schedule. By monitoring these treatment routines, the team ensures that each dog receives the care required for an effective and timely recovery.

Sterilization Drive

Pawsitive regularly organizes sterilization drives to support the long-term health and wellbeing of the campus dogs while also promoting responsible population management. Sterilization refers to neutering (for male dogs) and spaying (for female dogs) — veterinary procedures that prevent dogs from reproducing. For dogs living on the streets, sterilization plays a crucial role in reducing uncontrolled population growth, which in turn decreases competition for food, territory, and shelter.

For female dogs, spaying prevents repeated pregnancies and protects them from several health risks, including uterine infections and certain reproductive cancers. Street dogs that are not sterilized often experience frequent pregnancies under physically demanding conditions, which can weaken their health over time. For male dogs, neutering helps reduce aggressive behaviour, territorial fights, and roaming in search of mates — factors that often lead to injuries and road accidents. Sterilized males are generally calmer and less likely to engage in conflicts with other dogs.

In addition to improving the quality of life for individual animals, sterilization helps create more stable and healthier dog communities within a given area. By preventing the birth of vulnerable puppies who may struggle to survive on the streets, these drives contribute to a more humane and sustainable approach to managing urban stray dog populations.

Tick Removal

Pawsitive periodically conducts tick removal drives to protect the campus dogs from parasites that can seriously affect their health. Ticks are small blood-feeding parasites that attach themselves to a dog's skin and feed on its blood. If left untreated, tick infestations can cause skin irritation, weakness, and the transmission of serious diseases such as tick fever and other blood-borne infections.

During these drives, volunteers apply spot-on anti-tick treatments to the dogs. These medications are applied directly to the dog's skin and work by killing existing ticks while also preventing new infestations for a period of time.

Regular tick prevention is particularly important for dogs living outdoors, as they are frequently exposed to grassy areas and other animals where ticks are commonly found. Through these drives, Pawsitive helps ensure that the campus dogs remain healthier, more comfortable, and less vulnerable to tick-borne illnesses.

Vaccination Drive

Pawsitive conducts regular vaccination drives to protect the campus dogs from common and potentially life-threatening diseases. Vaccination is one of the most important aspects of community animal care, as it safeguards the health of individual dogs while also helping prevent the spread of infectious diseases among animals and humans.

For dogs living on the streets, vaccinations are especially important. Limited access to veterinary care and frequent interaction with other animals can increase their vulnerability to disease. By maintaining a consistent vaccination schedule, Pawsitive works to ensure that the campus dog population remains healthy and protected.

Puppies require multiple vaccinations during their early months to gradually build immunity. These typically include:

  • Nine-in-One Vaccine — administered in three doses to protect against diseases such as Canine Distemper Virus (CDV), Canine Parvovirus (CPV), Canine Adenovirus (CAV-1/CAV-2), Parainfluenza, and Leptospirosis.
  • Anti-Rabies Vaccine — administered in two doses once the puppy reaches the appropriate age to provide protection against rabies.
  • Canine Coronavirus (CCV) Vaccine — administered in two doses to protect against canine coronavirus, which primarily affects the gastrointestinal system of young dogs.

Adult dogs receive annual booster vaccinations to maintain immunity, including the Anti-Rabies vaccine and Nine-in-One vaccine administered once a year.

Visits to the Veterinary Clinic

Pawsitive ensures that the campus dogs receive timely veterinary care whenever medical attention is required. For routine health concerns — such as minor injuries, infections, or general check-ups — dogs are taken to a local veterinary clinic, where they are examined and treated as needed.

In cases of more serious medical conditions or emergencies, Pawsitive arranges visits to larger veterinary hospitals equipped to handle advanced procedures, including surgeries and specialized treatment. Members coordinate transportation, appointments, and follow-up care to ensure that each dog receives the medical attention it requires.
